1. Main points

Overall levels of crime showed falls in recent decades, but levels have remained broadly stable in recent years. While in the last year there has been no change in overall levels of crime, this hides variation seen in individual crime types. The latest figures show a mixed picture, with continued rises in some types of theft and fraud and falls in computer misuse. Consistent with the rising trend over recent years, there were increases in some of the less frequently occurring but higher-harm types of violence, including offences involving knives and sharp instruments.

Headline figures

Over recent quarters, estimates from the Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW) have indicated rises in theft. While the latest estimate showed no significant change in the last year, estimates of theft remain much lower than 20 years ago.

Police recorded crime figures give a reliable indication of trends in some types of offence involving theft that are thought to be well-reported and accurately recorded by the police. In the latest year these data show:

  • a 2% increase in vehicle offences
  • an 11% increase in robbery
  • a 3% decrease in burglaries

The CSEW shows that the level of lower-harm violent offences did not change. However, police recorded crime gives more insight into the lower-volume but higher-harm violence that the survey either does not cover or does not capture well. These data show:

  • an 8% increase in the number of offences involving knives or sharp instruments
  • a 3% increase in the number of offences involving firearms
  • a 4% decrease in the number of homicides

Many of these lower-volume, higher-harm types of violence tend to be concentrated in metropolitan police force areas.

The other changes seen in the main crime types measured by the CSEW were:

  • a 17% increase in fraud offences
  • a 21% decrease in computer misuse

Statistician's comment

Commenting on today’s figures, Mark Bangs from the Office for National Statistics Centre for Crime and Justice said:

“The picture of crime is a complex one. Overall levels of crime have remained steady, but this is not the case for all types of crime. For example, overall levels of violence have remained steady but we have seen increases in violent crimes involving knives and sharp instruments. We have seen increases in fraud and overall theft, but decreases in burglary following recent rises.”

The long-term picture of overall crime estimated by the Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW) shows continued falls since the mid-1990s. Despite some apparent fluctuation over the last two years, latest estimates for the year ending March 2019 show that there was no significant change in all CSEW crime (including fraud and computer misuse) experienced by adults aged 16 years and over compared with the previous year (11.2 million offences). The trend during this period has therefore been flat. However, it is important to look at individual crime types as the relatively stable overall picture hides variation both within and across crime types.

In contrast, the number of crimes recorded by the police increased by 8% in the last year (to 6.0 million offences). Over the longer-term, the trend in the number of police recorded crimes has been similar to that measured by the CSEW. However, the rises seen over recent years indicate a different trend to the CSEW, and reflect a combination of factors, including continuing improvements to recording processes and practices, more victims reporting crime, and genuine increases in some types of crime. Police recorded crime statistics do however provide a good measure of the crime-related demand on the police. It is not possible to estimate the relative contribution of each factor, but as a general measure of crime the CSEW is the preferred source.

! Warning Improvements to recording processes and practices by the police have made substantial contributions to rises in recorded crime over the last five years. This effect has been more pronounced for some crime types. The level and speed of improvement has varied across police forces and such activity could continue for some time.

For the offences and population that it covers, the CSEW provides the best measure of trends for overall violent crime, and particularly more common but less harmful offences (for example, assault with minor injury).

There were an estimated 1.3 million incidents of violence experienced by adults aged 16 years and over in the latest CSEW survey for the year ending March 2019. This figure has not changed significantly compared with last year and continues the relatively stable trend seen over the last five years, following long-term decreases since the peak in 1995.

The longer-term reductions in CSEW violent crime are reflected in research conducted by the Violence Research Group at Cardiff University, and also recent admissions data for NHS hospitals in England.

! Warning The effect of recording improvements by the police is thought to have been most pronounced in the relatively less harmful types of violent crime.

While the CSEW provides a good picture of the overall trend in violent crime, police recorded crime is a better measure of higher-harm but less common types of violence. Latest figures showed an 8% rise in offences involving knives or sharp instruments recorded by the police in the last year (to 43,516 offences), the highest number since recording began in March 2011. This latest rise is smaller than seen a year ago but continues the year-on-year increase in these offences since the year ending March 2014, following an initial downward trend.

The number of incidents of “assault with injury and assault with intent to cause serious harm” involving knives increased by 5% over the last year (to 20,182 offences), while the volume of robberies increased by 10% (to 18,462 offences). This continues the rise seen in both types of offence over recent years, and together these offences account for the large majority of all offences involving knives or sharp instruments recorded by the police.

Latest figures continue to indicate that knife or sharp instrument offences are concentrated in metropolitan areas, with London having the highest rate of 169 offences per 100,000 population. This compares to the England and Wales average of 80 offences per 100,000 population.

Other sources of data can help to provide insight into offences involving knives or sharp instruments. For example, provisional admissions data for NHS hospitals in England reported 5,024 admissions for assault by a sharp object between April 2018 and March 2019,, an increase of 1% compared with the previous year. Police recorded “possession of an article with a blade or point” offences rose by 21%, to 22,169 offences in the year ending March 2019.

! Warning Figures for offences involving knives or sharp instruments exclude Greater Manchester Police (GMP) as recent improvements to address previous undercounting of these offences means their data is not comparable with earlier figures.

Offences involving firearms recorded by the police increased by 3% (to 6,684 offences) in the last year. These offences have seen rises over the last five years, but the rate of increase has been slower over the last two years.

There were large increases in “unidentified firearms” and “other firearms” (31% and 40% respectively), which have driven the small increase in firearms offences, although recent improvements in police recording practices to better identify the involvement of a firearm may have contributed to these rises to some extent. However, this was balanced by the large volume decreases seen in offences involving imitation firearms and handguns.

Firearms offences are concentrated in urban areas, with London and the West Midlands each recording 24 offences per 100,000 population in the year ending March 2018 (latest data available). This compared with 11 offences per 100,000 population in England and Wales in the same period.

The total number of homicides recorded by the police fell by 4% in the last year (to 701 offences). This fall comes after an upward trend in homicides seen over the last four years, which contrasts with the downward trend over the previous decade. Despite this, the rate of homicide in the population remains very low, at 12 homicides per 1 million people. In part, this latest fall in homicides results from terrorist incidents having occurred in the previous year. It also reflects a decrease recorded by the Metropolitan Police in the latest year following a notably high number recorded in the previous year. The picture across other police forces was mixed.

The number of homicides where a knife or sharp instrument was involved decreased by 9% in the last year (to 260 offences). Of all recorded homicides in the latest data, 39% involved a knife or sharp instrument, the same proportion as the previous year. The recent reduction in such offences has been driven by falls seen in London (a 39% reduction to 67 homicides). There was also a 23% decrease in attempted murder offences in the latest year (to 1,033 offences). Much of this fall is because of the large number of attempted murder offences recorded in the previous year from the Manchester (235 offences) and London terrorist attacks (59 offences).

The CSEW is the most reliable indicator for long-term trends in the more common types of crime experienced by the general population, such as theft. The overall number of theft offences has fallen by 68% since the year ending December 1995, with long-term reductions in most categories of theft.

Although there was no overall change in the number of theft offences compared with the previous year, there was a 13% increase in theft offences (to 3.8 million offences) compared with the year ending March 2017 (3.3 million offences). It is too early to say whether this recent rise represents a change in the long-term downward trend in theft.

Some types of theft, such as domestic burglary and theft of a vehicle are less likely to be affected by the impact of recording improvements, as they are relatively well-reported to (and subsequently recorded by) the police.

Latest figures show that the number of burglary offences recorded by the police decreased by 3% in the last year (to 422,870 offences). This contrasts with rises in police recorded burglary offences seen in the years ending March 2016 and March 2017. CSEW domestic burglary has remained stable since the year ending March 2017.

Police recorded vehicle offences increased by 2% in the year ending March 2019 (to 468,315 offences), continuing the rising trend seen over the last three years. However, the size of the increase has reduced over the last year, due mainly to a smaller increase (8%, to 114,656 offences) in “theft or unauthorised taking of a vehicle” compared with previous years.

Rises in police recorded robbery offences were evident for the fourth consecutive year, with an increase of 11% (to 85,736 offences) from the previous year. This increase is likely to reflect a real change, as while recording improvements are likely to have contributed, the impact is thought to be less pronounced than for some other crime types.

9. Criminal damage and arson

The overall volume of criminal damage in the CSEW has not changed over the last year (1.1 million offences), following a sustained period of decrease over the last 10 years. However, latest figures do show a 17% increase in the subcategory “criminal damage to a vehicle” compared with the previous year (to 814,000 offences).

In comparison with CSEW findings, police recorded crime data for the year ending March 2019 showed a 3% decrease in criminal damage and arson (to 573,577 offences) and a 7% fall in criminal damage to a vehicle. The differences in criminal damage to a vehicle between the CSEW and police recorded crime could be partly explained by a relatively low reporting rate for this type of offence.

10. Fraud

The CSEW provides the best measure of fraud offences directly experienced by individuals in England and Wales. Findings for the year ending March 2019 estimated that the number of fraud incidents increased by 17% (to 3.8 million offences) from the previous year. The increase was driven mainly by a 15% increase in “bank and credit account fraud” (to 2.6 million offences).

Police recorded fraud incorporates fraud offences collated by the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau (NFIB) from Action Fraud (the public-facing national fraud and cybercrime reporting centre) and two industry bodies, Cifas and UK Finance (who report instances of fraud where their member organisations have been a victim). Latest figures showed a 9% rise in the total number of offences referred to the NFIB (to 693,418 offences) compared with the previous year.

Some differences were apparent when looking at the separate reporting bodies. Action Fraud reported a 12% rise (to 311,083 offences), Cifas reported a 13% rise (to 313,175 offences), and UK Finance reported an 18% fall (to 69,160 offences). These need to be interpreted in the context of differences in coverage and fraud types captured by the individual fraud reporting bodies.

! Warning Due to a temporary problem with NFIB systems, it is estimated that around 8,000 fraud offences referred by UK Finance have not yet been uploaded. The scale of the decrease compared with the previous year is likely to be substantially smaller than reported in these figures.

Fraud offences referred to the authorities make up a relatively small proportion of the overall volume of fraud. The CSEW captures a large volume of lower-harm cases that are less likely to have been reported to the authorities, while incidents of fraud referred to the NFIB will include reports from businesses and other organisations, and tend to mostly be focused on the more serious cases.

Additional fraud data collected by UK Finance via their CAMIS reporting database provide a broader range of bank account and plastic card frauds than those referred for police investigation to the NFIB. In the latest year, UK Finance reported 2.8 million cases of frauds involving UK-issued payment cards, remote banking and cheques via CAMIS. This is an increase of 39% from the previous year, driven largely by a rise in plastic card fraud, and particularly remote purchase fraud.

10. Fraud

The CSEW provides the better indication of the volume of computer misuse offences as it is able to capture some of the many incidents which go unreported. This can be seen by the large difference in volume of computer misuse offences reported by the CSEW (966,000 offences) and those referred to the NFIB by Action Fraud (21,321 offences). In addition, the two sources have different coverage which means it is not possible to make meaningful comparisons between them.

In the year ending March 2019, the CSEW estimated that computer misuse offences decreased by 21% from the previous year (to 966,000 offences; Table 6). This decrease was largely owing to a fall in “computer viruses” (down 38% to 444,000 offences).

All “computer misuse crime” referred to the NFIB by Action Fraud increased by 1% in the latest year ending March 2019 (to 21,321 offences). This rise was less pronounced than seen previously, due in part to a notable decrease of 37% over the latest year in computer viruses (to 4,177 offences). The overall rise was driven by increases in “hacking – extortion” (up 303% to 4,150 offences) and “hacking – social media and email” (up 3% to 8,340 offences).

4. Glossary

Fraud

Fraud involves a person dishonestly and deliberately deceiving a victim for personal gain of property or money, or causing loss or risk of loss to another. The majority of incidents fall under the legal definition of “Fraud by false representation” – where a person makes a representation that they know to be untrue or misleading (e.g. banking and payment card frauds and dating scams).

Homicide

Homicide includes the offences of murder, manslaughter, corporate manslaughter and infanticide.

Knife and sharp instrument offences

An offence is recorded by the police as involving a knife or sharp instrument when the weapon is present during the offence or the threat is believed to be real. The weapon does not necessarily have to be used. These data are based on a special collection covering selected offences.

Offences involving firearms

Offences involving a firearm include those where a firearm is fired, used as a blunt instrument, or used as a threat. Firearms include shotguns; handguns; rifles; imitation weapons such as BB guns or soft air weapons; other weapons such as CS gas, pepper spray and stun guns; and unidentified weapons. They exclude conventional air weapons, such as air rifles.

Overall theft offences

Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW) theft offences include all personal and household crime where items are stolen, including theft from the person; other theft of personal property; domestic burglary; vehicle-related theft; and bicycle theft.

Violent crime

Covers a range of offence types from minor assaults, such as pushing and shoving that result in no physical harm, to murder. This includes offences where the victim was intentionally stabbed, punched, kicked, pushed or jostled, as well as offences where the victim was threatened with violence, regardless of injury.

A comprehensive guide to crime types and definitions can be found in the User Guide to Crime Statistics for England and Wales.

5. Measuring this data

This page is part of our quarterly crime collection, which relies on two main sources of data.

Crime Survey of England and Wales
The CSEW is a face-to-face victimisation survey, which asks people resident in households in England and Wales about their experiences of a selected range of offences in the 12 months prior to the interview.

Police recorded crime
Police recorded crime is the number of notifiable crimes reported to and recorded by the 44 police forces in England and Wales. For some types of crime, due to ongoing changes in police recording practices, an increase in the number of offences recorded by the police is unlikely to indicate a real rise in these types of crime. Police recorded crime data are not designated as National Statistics.

More information on both these sources can be found in the User guide to crime statistics for England and Wales.

More information on the methodology can be found in the Crime in England and Wales Quality and Methodology Information report.

6. Strengths and limitations

Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW)

The CSEW is a large nationally representative sample survey that has used a consistent methodology over time. The survey covers crimes not reported to the police and is not affected by changes in police recording practice; therefore, it is a reliable measure of long-term trends.

The CSEW does not cover crimes against businesses and those not resident in households (for example, residents of institutions and visitors), and headline estimates exclude offences that are difficult to estimate robustly (such as sexual offences) or that have no victim who can be interviewed (for example, homicides and drug offences). In addition, the survey is subject to error associated with sampling and respondents recalling past events. Potential time lag between occurrence of the crime and survey data collection means that the survey is not a good measure of emerging trends.

Police recorded crime

Police recorded crime has wider offence coverage and population coverage than the CSEW. It is the primary source of local crime statistics and is a good measure of offences that are well-reported to and well-recorded by the police, as well as lower volume crimes (for example, homicide). In addition, the time lag between occurrence of crime and reporting results tends to be short, providing an indication of emerging trends.

On the other hand, police recorded crime excludes offences that are not reported to, or not recorded by, the police and does not include less serious offences dealt with by magistrates’ courts (for example, motoring offences). Trends can be influenced by changes in recording practices or police activity as well as public reporting of crime, making it difficult to make long-term comparisons. There are also concerns about the quality of recording – crimes may not be recorded consistently across police forces and so the true level of recorded crime may be understated.
