2. Main points

  • Following long-term decreases since the peak in 1995, latest findings from the Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW) show that the level of high-volume but lower-harm violent offences (for example, violence without injury and assault with minor injury) continues to remain stable.
  • Latest police recorded figures show continued increases in lower-volume but higher-harm violent offences involving the use of weapons, with an 8% increase in offences involving knives or sharp instruments and a 3% increase in offences involving firearms.
  • In contrast, following a period of increases over the last four years, homicides recorded by the police have decreased by 4%.
  • These higher-harm violent offences tend to be concentrated in metropolitan police force areas such as London, the West Midlands, West Yorkshire and Greater Manchester.

Statistician's comment

Commenting on today’s figures, Mark Bangs from the Office for National Statistics Centre for Crime and Justice said:

“The picture of crime is a complex one. Overall levels of crime have remained steady, but this is not the case for all types of crime. For example, overall levels of violence have remained steady but we have seen increases in violent crimes involving knives and sharp instruments. We have seen increases in fraud and overall theft, but decreases in burglary following recent rises.”

For the offences and population that it covers, the CSEW provides the best measure of trends for overall violent crime, and particularly more common but less harmful offences (for example, assault with minor injury).

There were an estimated 1.3 million incidents of violence experienced by adults aged 16 years and over in the latest CSEW survey for the year ending March 2019. This figure has not changed significantly compared with last year and continues the relatively stable trend seen over the last five years, following long-term decreases since the peak in 1995.

The longer-term reductions in CSEW violent crime are reflected in research conducted by the Violence Research Group at Cardiff University, and also recent admissions data for NHS hospitals in England.

! Warning The effect of recording improvements by the police is thought to have been most pronounced in the relatively less harmful types of violent crime.

While the CSEW provides a good picture of the overall trend in violent crime, police recorded crime is a better measure of higher-harm but less common types of violence. Latest figures showed an 8% rise in offences involving knives or sharp instruments recorded by the police in the last year (to 43,516 offences), the highest number since recording began in March 2011. This latest rise is smaller than seen a year ago but continues the year-on-year increase in these offences since the year ending March 2014, following an initial downward trend.

The number of incidents of “assault with injury and assault with intent to cause serious harm” involving knives increased by 5% over the last year (to 20,182 offences), while the volume of robberies increased by 10% (to 18,462 offences). This continues the rise seen in both types of offence over recent years, and together these offences account for the large majority of all offences involving knives or sharp instruments recorded by the police.

Latest figures continue to indicate that knife or sharp instrument offences are concentrated in metropolitan areas, with London having the highest rate of 169 offences per 100,000 population. This compares to the England and Wales average of 80 offences per 100,000 population.

Other sources of data can help to provide insight into offences involving knives or sharp instruments. For example, provisional admissions data for NHS hospitals in England reported 5,024 admissions for assault by a sharp object between April 2018 and March 2019,, an increase of 1% compared with the previous year. Police recorded “possession of an article with a blade or point” offences rose by 21%, to 22,169 offences in the year ending March 2019.

! Warning Figures for offences involving knives or sharp instruments exclude Greater Manchester Police (GMP) as recent improvements to address previous undercounting of these offences means their data is not comparable with earlier figures.

Offences involving firearms recorded by the police increased by 3% (to 6,684 offences) in the last year. These offences have seen rises over the last five years, but the rate of increase has been slower over the last two years.

There were large increases in “unidentified firearms” and “other firearms” (31% and 40% respectively), which have driven the small increase in firearms offences, although recent improvements in police recording practices to better identify the involvement of a firearm may have contributed to these rises to some extent. However, this was balanced by the large volume decreases seen in offences involving imitation firearms and handguns.

Firearms offences are concentrated in urban areas, with London and the West Midlands each recording 24 offences per 100,000 population in the year ending March 2018 (latest data available). This compared with 11 offences per 100,000 population in England and Wales in the same period.

The total number of homicides recorded by the police fell by 4% in the last year (to 701 offences). This fall comes after an upward trend in homicides seen over the last four years, which contrasts with the downward trend over the previous decade. Despite this, the rate of homicide in the population remains very low, at 12 homicides per 1 million people. In part, this latest fall in homicides results from terrorist incidents having occurred in the previous year. It also reflects a decrease recorded by the Metropolitan Police in the latest year following a notably high number recorded in the previous year. The picture across other police forces was mixed.

The number of homicides where a knife or sharp instrument was involved decreased by 9% in the last year (to 260 offences). Of all recorded homicides in the latest data, 39% involved a knife or sharp instrument, the same proportion as the previous year. The recent reduction in such offences has been driven by falls seen in London (a 39% reduction to 67 homicides). There was also a 23% decrease in attempted murder offences in the latest year (to 1,033 offences). Much of this fall is because of the large number of attempted murder offences recorded in the previous year from the Manchester (235 offences) and London terrorist attacks (59 offences).

5. Glossary

Assault with injury and assault with intent to cause serious harm

Offences include injury resulting in permanent disability; more than minor permanent disfigurement; broken bones; fractured skull; compound fractures; substantial loss of blood; internal injury; lengthy treatment or serious psychiatric injury (based on expert evidence); and shock (when accompanied by expert psychological evidence).

Homicide

Homicide includes the offences of murder, manslaughter, corporate manslaughter and infanticide.

Knife and sharp instrument offences

An offence is recorded by the police as involving a knife or sharp instrument when the weapon is present during the offence or the threat is believed to be real. The weapon does not necessarily have to be used. These data are based on a special collection covering selected offences.

Offences involving firearms

Offences involving a firearm include those where a firearm is fired, used as a blunt instrument, or used as a threat. Firearms include shotguns; handguns; rifles; imitation weapons such as BB guns or soft air weapons; other weapons such as CS gas, pepper spray and stun guns; and unidentified weapons. They exclude conventional air weapons, such as air rifles.

Violent crime

Covers a range of offence types from minor assaults, such as pushing and shoving that result in no physical harm, to murder. This includes offences where the victim was intentionally stabbed, punched, kicked, pushed or jostled, as well as offences where the victim was threatened with violence, regardless of injury.

A comprehensive guide to crime types and definitions can be found in the User Guide to Crime Statistics for England and Wales.

6. Measuring this data

This page is part of our quarterly crime collection, which relies on two main sources of data.

Crime Survey of England and Wales
The CSEW is a face-to-face victimisation survey, which asks people resident in households in England and Wales about their experiences of a selected range of offences in the 12 months prior to the interview.

Police recorded crime
Police recorded crime is the number of notifiable crimes reported to and recorded by the 44 police forces in England and Wales. For some types of crime, due to ongoing changes in police recording practices, an increase in the number of offences recorded by the police is unlikely to indicate a real rise in these types of crime. Police recorded crime data are not designated as National Statistics.

More information on both these sources can be found in the User guide to crime statistics for England and Wales.

More information on the methodology can be found in the Crime in England and Wales Quality and Methodology Information report.

7. Strengths and limitations

Crime Survey for England and Wales (CSEW)

The CSEW is a large nationally representative sample survey that has used a consistent methodology over time. The survey covers crimes not reported to the police and is not affected by changes in police recording practice; therefore, it is a reliable measure of long-term trends.

The CSEW does not cover crimes against businesses and those not resident in households (for example, residents of institutions and visitors), and headline estimates exclude offences that are difficult to estimate robustly (such as sexual offences) or that have no victim who can be interviewed (for example, homicides and drug offences). In addition, the survey is subject to error associated with sampling and respondents recalling past events. Potential time lag between occurrence of the crime and survey data collection means that the survey is not a good measure of emerging trends.

Police recorded crime

Police recorded crime has wider offence coverage and population coverage than the CSEW. It is the primary source of local crime statistics and is a good measure of offences that are well-reported to and well-recorded by the police, as well as lower volume crimes (for example, homicide). In addition, the time lag between occurrence of crime and reporting results tends to be short, providing an indication of emerging trends.

On the other hand, police recorded crime excludes offences that are not reported to, or not recorded by, the police and does not include less serious offences dealt with by magistrates’ courts (for example, motoring offences). Trends can be influenced by changes in recording practices or police activity as well as public reporting of crime, making it difficult to make long-term comparisons. There are also concerns about the quality of recording – crimes may not be recorded consistently across police forces and so the true level of recorded crime may be understated.
