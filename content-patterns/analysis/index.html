Analysis

Writing your analysis

When writing stories about statistics, focus on the 'story' – what's interesting, noteworthy or important.

Don't couch the story in caveats and warnings. Where there are warnings about the reliability of the data, say so as briefly as possible. Avoid repeating caveats throughout your stories.

Lead point

This should be the most important story about your new data. When thinking about importance, consider:

  • what is the most interesting thing about the new data?
  • is that something that most users will find interesting?
  • is it statistically significant?
  • could anything about this point be misleading?

The lead point consists of a headline, a subtitle, and can include a visual (a chart, for example):

A headline

This should be less than 90 characters, summarise the most important story in your summary, and accurately describe any accompanying visuals or text.

Subtitle

Describe the statistical measure, geographic coverage, and time period as briefly as possible.

Visual

This will typically be a chart, and should back up the message in the headline. A subtitle should explain the geography and time period.

Where a chart could be misleading, or isn't appropriate, consider using a quote instead. The quote should be 50 to 60 words, and clearly attributed to an ONS employee, with their job title.

Remember that you can add a quote alongside your lead point (see supporting information) , which can be useful if you need to add nuance.

Supporting information

Alongside the lead point, supporting information can add nuance, or extra detail about the lead point. Supporting information can take the form of a quote or bullet points.

A quote

This is particularly useful where the lead point is complex, or requires further explanation.

The quote can be 50 to 60 words long, and should be attributed to an ONS employee and their job title.

The quote should not contradict the headline or visual from your lead point.

Bullet points

Where there are three or four short points to make which give more information about the summary.

Main points

A main point is trend or finding from the data which is worth reporting. It should answer a question the user might have about your statistics.

Main findings consist of a title and commentary. Add visuals, such as charts, tables, or interactives to backup each point. Use the warning pattern to draw attention to essential limitations or uncertainty which might affect the way people interpret a statistics.

Aim for 5 or 6 main points on a page, adding up to no more than 800 words. If you know that users want to know more, consider adding more pages to your release.

Communicating clearly with a small number of words can be difficult and time consuming, but we know it's what our users want. The content design team can help if you're finding anything difficult.

Do: Don't:
Use descriptive titles – users need to know what the text beneath a headline will tell them. Use your data source as the headline – you can mention the source, but find the thing that users will find interesting about that data source.
Say something useful and interesting in each point. Use points just to report on the findings from different sources of data.
Create a new point for each new "idea". Try to talk about more than one thing in each point.
Base your points on themes, not data sources. Use more than six points on a page. If you need to say more, consider whether you need another page.
Write extra points if there's nothing to say – it's fine if summaries are short.

Title

Describe the finding as clearly as you can in the shortest number of words. When people read web pages, their eyes scan shapes and headings first, trying to find which bits of the page are going to be most useful to them. Even at a glance, titles should say something meaningful, and give a clear idea what the following commentary is about.

For example

Police recorded rises in knife and gun crime, mainly in large cities

Be clear if there's any uncertainty in the point you're making, or you need to add context, but keep it simple. Complex terms or awkward phrasing reduced people's ability to understand and retain information.

For example

Crime Survey shows long-term drop in violent crime but little change in recent years

If you can't say it simply, consider whether it should be a main point – can you highlight something more useful?

The title should be less than 20 words (about 90 characters), to avoid running over two lines on a desktop.

Commentary

Use text, charts and/or tables to give more detail and context to your title.

Your text should be in concise, plain English and written with the user in mind. Consider what is the most important information that supports your title.

If you're not sure something is going to be useful or interesting, don't include it. Users can still find the data in datasets and further detail can be explored in an accompanying article.

The first paragraph of each section must contain the most important fact. Use further paragraphs and figures to add more detail and context to broaden the user's understanding.

Alerts

Alerts are designed to grab the user's attention. They can be inserted anywhere in your commentary to bring attention to very important information.

Do Don't
Use alerts to highlight essential limitations of the data Use too many warnings – it disrupts the reading experience and makes it difficult to understand the content
Add context to help users avoid misinterpreting the data Use too many words
Make your alerts short, and clear Use complicated language – when text is hard to understand, people retain less information
Lead with the alert – write your finding first, then add context. Testing has shown that users find it confusing when we write warnings before commentary.

For example

Police recorded crime data are not designated as National Statistics.

Charts and interactives

Use charts to deliver a clear message visually. If a chart doesn't tell its own "story", or doesn't fit with the narrative of the point you're writing, consider whether it's really needed.

Use interactive charts and maps sparingly when they can bring an extra dimension to your data.

Contact the ONS Data Visualisation Centre (datavis@ons.gov.uk) for advice and guidance on interactive charts.

Chart titles and subtitles

Charts should have a main title and a statistical subtitle, be concise and in sentence case.

The main title should be descriptive, and tell the trend of the data or highlight the main story. Try to limit the number of words to no more than 10. This should make the description easier to read and avoid the text wrapping onto several lines, especially on mobile devices. If you need to add context or detail to the chart, use annotations or support with your analysis.

For example

Figure 1: Highest overall employment rate since 1971

The statistical subtitle should be as short as possible and must include the:

  • statistical measure
  • geographic coverage
  • time period

For example

UK employment rates (aged 16 to 64 years), seasonally adjusted, 1971 to 2018

When writing your chart title and analysis, take care to:

  • avoid repeating section headings
  • avoid repeating what you have written about in your analysis
  • add further context and explanation of the chart's message in your main text

Sources

Figures and tables must provide a source, in the following format:

[ORGANISATION], [PUBLICATION OR SOURCE OF DATA]

For example

Office for National Statistics, Annual Survey of Hours and Earnings

Footnotes

Avoid chart footnotes. If extra information is needed:

  • annotate the chart
  • include the information in the commentary accompanying the chart

Tables

Tables can capture a great deal of data but often aren't the clearest or most transparent way of presenting the statistics or telling the story – a chart is usually clearer, for example.

Show only the data you need to evidence the point you're making. If a user wants all of the data, they can download it. Only use tables in your bulletin if there is a strong justification for doing so.
